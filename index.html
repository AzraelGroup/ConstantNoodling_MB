Good people still live in Crime Alley.

Nov 16, 2022

There are a few crystal clear memories I have from when I was 7 years old.

Being jealous of this girl in my second grade classroom for always winning the art student of month award.

The small copse of trees in the landscaping of the church at my school that I would hide in and pretend I was a dinosaur.

Hearing Metallica’s Ride The Lightning album in Mr. Ferguson’s tape deck as he hauled all the kids in our cub scout pack to camp in his blue passenger.

But one memory I have that I’ve been thinking a lot about lately is my mom telling me about a new Batman cartoon that was going to come out the first week in September in 1992. I was going to turn 8 at the end of September…and one could think that a 7/8 year old would be thrilled to hear about a Batman cartoon.

I was initially was not thrilled…in fact, I thought it would be dumb.

My parents were pretty loose regard what movies I could watch when I was young…so I already seen Alien, Predator, and the original Tim Burton Batman movie. Predator was honestly the only nightmare-inducing one…heat vision was a lot to take in as a little kid. But Batman…wow…what a movie! It was way better than that dumb 60’s TV Show with Adam West and Burt Ward (I originally thought that era of Batman was cool…that with watching The Munsters and Gilligan’s Island made up much of my live action TV consumption a few years prior.)

So with the pedestal of 1989’s Batman held being placed and the depths of lows being the 60’s show…even as a 7 year old, my mind went to thinking a cartoon is going to suck. So I was initially against the notion of the Batman, The Animated Series to be something I wanted to watch. But a week later with nothing else on TV, I decided to watch it.

Neurons fired, quasars collided, black holes consumed galaxies, a telegraph began recieving communications and printing its message. Batman, The Animated Series was an inflection point…There was a before BTAS and after BTAS. From then on I was hooked…the stories, the drama, the characters, the portrayals…it was every bit as dark and menacing that I remembered or attributed to the Tim Burton movie, but with more gravitas. This Batman was someone who could scare the souls out of criminals, cops, and anyone in his way. The voice of the Bat had the angst, weight, depth, confidence, and tone that for me that was the voice of Batman.

Over the years, I consumed everything made by the DC Animated Universe. That iconic voice acting of Kevin Conroy remained a cornerstone of the character. Batman Mask of the Phantasm is still in my top five favorite movies of all time. There are innumerable memories of me watching that original show and all of the spinnoffs, conversations I’ve had with friends in the classrooms, on the job, in bars just discussing the mythos and stories of Batman…all with the voice of the Bat always echoing in my head.

When I learned of Kevin Conroy’s passing earlier this month…I sat at my desk and just felt empty. What Kevin brought to Batman was more than just a voice, he brought the characters to life as both Bruce Wayne and Batman. It’s difficult to articulate just how Kevin fleshed out the characters simultaneously being separate and connected. I was instantly drawn to his portrayal of the character when I heard him speak. The flood of memories of episodes, interviews, pivotal scenes, all flooded back to me from the initial emptiness…as if I was standing at the abyss and instead of the abyss blinking…it gave me back all of the memories and love for Batman, the animated series, and for Kevin.

My son is four…he has several Batman themed items in his room and we’re going to watch Batman Mask of the Phantasm this weekend. Even at four years old and all of the other Batman media that we have today…he watched the episode “Almost Got ‘Im” and said “ooh that’s Batman.” Damn straight kiddo, damn straight.

I am glad my snobbish 7 year old critique didn’t hold me back from watching that new Batman cartoon in 1992…it would have never openned doors to wonder, excitement, and appreciation that I hold dearly today. I am so thankful for and appreciative of Kevin Conroy’s dedication, humanity, thoughtfulness, and portryal of Batman. Beyond his work, Kevin’s love and involvement with the throngs of fans goes beyond all expectations. I hope he knows just much of an impact he has made on us fans.

Rest In Peace Kevin Conroy. You will always be my Batman.

Nostalgia About Painting

Apr 28, 2022

Let’s go back to 2002. I was a junior in high school and I was playing drums in my new band, From Their Eyes. We were doing the bar and local venue circuit and as a metalcore band in Central Pennsylvania, we were doing well beyond expectations of what this area could offer. Maybe it was the recent success of another local metalcore band, August Burns Red, that contributed to a favorable scene. So we’re playing shows regularly on the weekends, and since I am the youngest member of the band and I’m still in high school, I am also attending school during the week. Squeeze in a schedule working at my local Sheetz and practicing at band HQ in New Cumberland…I’m pretty damn busy.

I wasn’t sure how serious the band was going to be…we had all the hallmarks of commitment, but I was still thinking about a future with more stability….so obviously…that meant art. Because…what could be more stable, right? lol.

After high school, I enrolled at my local community college (which we joked was basically 13th grade for our high school) and started taking classes in fine art, specifically painting. With a more open schedule, my band played more shows and I was painting for class. I felt pretty bohemian in my life at this point. But surprisingly, the band took off and we started playing more shows in larger cities and we joined Warped Tour as one of the myriad smaller bands on the smaller stages. I was at an inflection point…do music or paint.

I chose music. For three years that was my life; touring, traveling, sleeping poorly, and getting paid shit.

In 2006, I decided to go back to school after the band dissolved. This time for design. I was still dreaming about being an artist, I just knew I wasn’t going to become the next Gerald Brom so design was for me. However, many in my class were also huge art nerds and while our tastes differed dramatically we all enjoyed being exposed to different artists. During this time a guy in my class that started hanging out with me a lot introduced me to Shawn Barber with one short, shitty YouTube video of a studio tour (mind you, this was back when YouTube was either just purchased by Google or right before.) The video begins with Shawn painting a portrait of one of my favorite singers of all time, Davey Havok of AFI. So I was hooked on this guy and his style and technique. That was back in 2006.

Well…looks like that shitty studio tour video still exists and you should check it out. Seeing this video…makes we want to dig out all of my old oil paints again.
